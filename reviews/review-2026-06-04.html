Best Practices for Preparing a Medical Manuscript for Journal Submission

Published: 2026-06-04  |  Journal: Global Journal of Medical Research  |  Publisher: Knowledge Framework Consulting  |  Author: Abhishek Bansal

Introduction

Publishing a medical manuscript in a peer‑reviewed journal is the cornerstone of academic communication. Yet, even experienced authors face rejection due to poor manuscript preparation, unclear writing, or failure to follow journal guidelines. This review synthesizes current best practices to help researchers improve manuscript quality and increase the likelihood of acceptance.

Pre‑Writing Phase: Choosing the Right Journal and Understanding Guidelines

Before writing a single word, selecting the appropriate journal is critical. Authors should consider the journal's scope, audience, impact factor, and indexing status (e.g., PubMed, Scopus). Once a target journal is chosen, carefully reading the "Instructions for Authors" is mandatory. Many rejections occur simply because formatting requirements were ignored. Key elements include word limits, reference style, abstract structure, and ethical declarations.

Structuring the Manuscript: IMRaD Format

The standard IMRaD structure (Introduction, Methods, Results, and Discussion) remains the backbone of most medical articles. The Introduction should clearly state the research question and its importance. The Methods section must provide enough detail for reproducibility, including study design, participants, interventions, and statistical analyses. The Results section should present findings objectively without interpretation. Finally, the Discussion should interpret the results in the context of existing literature, acknowledge limitations, and suggest future directions.

Writing Style and Clarity

Medical writing demands precision, brevity, and clarity. Avoid jargon that may confuse reviewers from other disciplines. Use active voice where possible, keep sentences short, and define acronyms on first use. The abstract is often the only part read by editors and reviewers; it must succinctly summarize the entire study. A well‑written cover letter that explains the significance of the work can also influence editorial decisions.

Data Presentation and Visuals

Tables and figures should be self‑explanatory and not merely repeat the text. Ensure all images are high resolution and all charts are properly labeled. Statistical data must be reported according to standard guidelines (e.g., CONSORT for trials, STROBE for observational studies). Transparency in data reporting enhances credibility and reduces the risk of desk rejection.

Ethical Considerations and Plagiarism

All manuscripts must comply with ethical guidelines, including patient consent, institutional review board approval, and conflict‑of‑interest disclosures. Plagiarism—whether intentional or accidental—is grounds for immediate rejection and can damage a researcher's reputation. Using plagiarism‑detection software before submission is highly recommended.

Conclusion

Successful manuscript preparation requires meticulous planning, adherence to journal guidelines, and clear, ethical writing. By following these best practices, authors can significantly reduce the risk of rejection and contribute meaningfully to the medical literature.
